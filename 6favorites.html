My Favorites

Movies matter.

They teach us lessons and are reliable comforts in our time of need.

They set examples of healthy relationships, show the journey of life obstacles, help define our values and virtues, bridge different experiences and perspectives, teach us how to be brave and kind, warn us of how our actions can shape our society, allow room for needed catharsis, and so much more.

Everything Everywhere All At Once

Everything Everywhere All At Once is a heartbreaking story of love and philosophy; it asks and answers the question everyone wants the answer to: What is the point of life?

The audience follows a ordinairy family who migrated from China to run a laundromat. Our protagonist, Evelyn, is catapulted into an alternate universe where she learns that she is living the worst version of her life where her business and familiar relationships fail greatly.

In particullar, her distraught relationship with her daughter is the seed of the chaos in the multiverse and only she, the "worst Evelyn", has the power of restoring.

We witness an action-packed philosophical debate on how much anything we do in life matters. We suffer with the characters, and experiene a rebalance in our soul as Evelyn restorse balance to the multiverse.

Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind

Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind has, in my opinion, some of the most beautiful and honest writing told in an amazinlyg distorted and unique way.

Charlie Kaufman, who wrote the original screenplay, tells a story of two people who seem to reflect the anxiety, fear, anger, and loneliness in all of us.

It is a story about feeling desperation to connect with someone in a real way.

After creating a hearty bond with the characters, Kaufman takes us on a painful journey of loss and leaves a bitter-sweet ending that serves as a reminder of a great and real life lesson: it is better to have loved and lost, than not loved at all.

Kaufman tells us that to love is to live.

Roma

Roma is a movie about the overwhelmingly simplicity of reality. It shows what is't like to be a woman in 1970's Mexico.

It is a film that feels personal, real, and shatters the thin line between fiction and documentary- and in a good way. Roma is based on the nanny from the director's childhood who raised him.

It focuses on relationships, especially the ones of two different woman who go through very similar experiences.

It is a story about family and love, change and loss through a film that has a slower pace, and an appriciation for a culturally different lifestyle.

La La Land

La La Land is a movie about chasing your dreams, and the sacrifices one must make to get there.

Some of the tools used to create the story is the use of color and light.

In the beginning, when Mia is actively chasing her dreams, her wardrobe is bright and colorful, and usually contrasted by a washed out background. Towards the end, as she gives up her hopes, her colors become lifeless.

Another tool used is the direction of camera movement. The camera is lively and fast paced during the scene where Mia and Sebastian are in the jazz club.

The rapid camera panning drives a sense of excitement and joy as the two play a call-and-response game of sorts.

Rahter than experiencing this as an observer, we feel like a participant, creating a bond between us and the characters that will be vital to create empathy and hard-hitting moments throughout the story.
